High‑Score Strategies

Targeted techniques for the most heavily weighted tasks
🎯 Focus on Read Aloud, Repeat Sentence, Highlight Incorrect Words, Write from Dictation & Reorder Paragraphs 📘 Bonus material

To achieve top marks on the PTE Academic exam, you need targeted strategies for the highest‑scoring tasks. Focusing your efforts on Read Aloud, Repeat Sentence, Highlight Incorrect Words, and Write from Dictation will yield the highest returns, as these tasks carry the most weight across multiple sections.

Read Aloud

Speaking & Reading
  • The 3‑Second Rule: The microphone closes automatically after 3 seconds of silence. Once the recording bar starts, you must begin speaking immediately.
  • The No‑Correction Rule: If you mispronounce, skip, or stumble over a word, never stop to correct yourself. A single mispronunciation only costs a fraction of a point in content. However, pausing to fix it breaks your oral fluency, which carries a much heavier penalty. Keep your speech moving forward at a steady pace.
  • Chunking: Use your 30 to 40‑second preparation time to scan the text for difficult words and map out natural pausing points at commas and periods. Read the text in small, logical phrases rather than word‑by‑word.
📖 Source: Language Academy PTE Speaking Guide →
💡 Practice daily: pick a short news paragraph, read it aloud once for practice, then once for real, timed — no pauses, no restarts.

Repeat Sentence

Speaking & Listening
  • The 50% Content Trick: Many test‑takers lose points by trying to repeat long sentences (12–16 words) word‑for‑word, which causes them to hesitate or stutter. In PTE, reproducing at least 50% of the words in the correct order still awards you 2 out of 3 content points. If you get 50% of the content perfectly correct with 100% fluent delivery, you will score much higher than trying for 100% content and stuttering.
  • Close the Eyes: Close your eyes while listening to the audio to block out visual distractions in the test room. Focus entirely on the meaning and rhythm of the sentence rather than trying to memorize a list of words.
  • Copy the Voice: Match the speaker's natural pauses, rising and falling tones, and stressed words. You do not need to change your natural accent, but copying the speaker's rhythm helps the AI understand your speech pattern.
📖 Source: Dream English Repeat Sentence Guide →
💡 Home exercise: read random sentences from a magazine, then repeat them back immediately — no writing allowed, purely ear‑to‑mouth.

Highlight Incorrect Words

Listening & Reading
Negative Marking Caution: This task penalises incorrect guesses. Every wrong click deducts a point from your score. If you are not 100% sure a word was different, do not click it. Leftover points cannot drop below zero for a single question.
  • The Finger‑Tracking Method: Use your mouse cursor or your finger to physically trace the words on the screen as the speaker says them. This keeps your eyes from jumping ahead or falling behind the speed of the audio track.
📖 Source: EEC Global Listening Tips →
💡 Practise with a partner: read a paragraph aloud but deliberately change 4‑5 words; your partner listens and circles the mismatches.

Write from Dictation

Listening & Writing
  • The First‑Letter Method: While listening, do not type. Instead, use your erasable notepad to write down only the first letter of each word you hear.
    Example: "The economic policy has been revised" becomes "T e p h b r".
    Immediately after the audio stops, use these letters as memory triggers to type the full sentence into the box.
  • Spelling and Punctuation Check: You must start your typed sentence with a capital letter and end it with a single period. Check for common spelling traps, especially words with double consonants (like accommodation or committee) and silent letters (like environment).
📖 Source: OnePTE Dictation Guide →
💡 Home exercise: read a sentence once at natural speed, then type it from memory word‑for‑word. Start short, then increase length.

Reorder Paragraphs

Reading
  • Find the Independent Sentence: Search for the one sentence that can stand alone without relying on other sentences for context. This sentence will not start with transition words (like however, therefore, furthermore, on the other hand) or pronouns referring to unnamed entities (like he, she, they, this, these). This is your lead sentence.
  • Nouns to Pronouns: Look for a sentence that introduces a full proper noun (e.g., "Dr. John Smith"), and match it with the sentence that later refers to them with a pronoun (e.g., "He then conducted..."). The noun sentence will always come first.
  • Chronological Order: Look for dates, years, or logical progress markers (e.g., "In 1995..." followed by "A decade later..."). Arrange these chronologically.
📖 Source: (based on official PTE reading strategies)
💡 Home exercise: take a short paragraph, cut it into 4‑5 strips, mix them up, then put them back in the correct order. Practise with different text types.
